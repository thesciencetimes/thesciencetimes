The Fight Against Alzheimer’s

Hyun Song Lee (Julie)

January 2024

Imagine living without a sense of who you are, who you used to be, and who your loved ones are. Your soul erodes bit by bit until there is nothing left.

Alzheimer's is a disease characterized by memory loss and impaired reasoning. It's scary because there is no cure; once a person is diagnosed with Alzheimer's, it progressively worsens until they lose most of their memories. Many drugs have been developed to slow down its progression, but they are ineffective in the long run due to the rapid changes occurring inside the patient’s brain. Since treating Alzheimer’s is challenging, it’s important to take preventive measures early on.

What Causes Alzheimer's?

In a healthy human brain, millions of neurons transmit signals to one another. These signals cause the chemicals at the end of the neuron to be released and spread across the synapse to a neighboring neuron. This signal flow is why we are able to see, move, think, feel, and memorize. However, in individuals with Alzheimer's, this neural communication breaks down.

During communication, neurons release small peptides called amyloid beta. Normally, amyloid beta is cleared away by microglia, which regulates the release of chemicals and phagocytizes waste products. However, when too much amyloid beta is released or not enough is cleared away, it begins to pile up in the synapse and form amyloid plaques. These plaques are dangerous because they hinder the communication between neurons. In addition, this disruption hyper-activates microglia and initiates an inflammatory response. Inflammation is a natural defense mechanism, but in the context of Alzheimer's disease, this heightened immune response contributes to the ongoing damage and deterioration of neural tissue.

Another marker for Alzheimer’s Disease is tau tangles. Tau is found inside neurons and is responsible for maintaining neuron structure. However, in Alzheimer’s brain, tau molecules stick to one another and form threads that join together to create massive tangles. These tangles damage the neuron structures and hinder their abilities to communicate with each other.

As communication between neurons falters, the brain experiences a decline in cognitive function. For example, people might have a harder time finding the right words and making sound decisions. The cumulative impact of these disruptions ultimately contributes to the progression of Alzheimer’s.
